## Velella is a CoastFI Scenario Planner.

---

Velella is an app built around a simple premise: there is no such thing as a "CoastFI Number." 



#### What is CoastFire?

The notion of a "CoastFI Number" comes from the idea of a "FIRE" or "Financial Independence, Early Retirement)" number. 

For simple "FIRE," we are simply saving up to a big number, then we can retire completely. The general rule is to multiply your annual expenses by 25 to get your "FIRE number." If you spend $80,000 a year, multiply that by 25 to get $2,000,000. Your goal—your "FIRE number" is $2m. If you can save up that amount, you can retire. Simple!

CoastFI is much different. With CoastFI, we don't simply pick a "retirement date" and work hard to achieve it. Instead, we think of life in phases: some phases focused on "accumulation" (work hard, make a lot, invest a lot), some focused on "decumulation" (don't work at all, just spend like a normal "retirement") and "coast" years. 

| Phase of life | Wage Income                                                  | Portfolio                                                    |
| ------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Accumulation  | Work hard, try to maximize income so you can invest more.    | Investing as much as possible and letting the portfolio grow. |
| Coast         | Just make enough money to cover your expenses. No need to invest anymore. | Let the portfolio grow. Don't pull from it, don't add to it. |
| Decumulate    | Retire (stop making income).                                 | Fund life's expenses by pulling funds from your portfolio.   |

#### A simple example: Jack and Eve

Let's imagine a fictional couple: Jack and Eve. In their 20's Jack and Eve start working. They know that they'll need $2m to retire. They also know that market returns compound over time, so the more they invest when they're young, the greater their wealth will compound. They have determined (using an online calculator that is NOT Velella) that their "CoastFI number" is $1m. If they hit that number, they can stop investing. 

So they go through phases:

***They Accumulate*** in their 20's and 30's, they make a combines income of $150,000, but they only spend about $80,000 during the year. They invest the other $70,000. By their mid thirties, they have a portfolio of about $1m. 

***They Coast*** in their late 30's, 40's and 50's. By their mid thirties, they feel pretty comfortable about the $1m capital base in their portfolio—it isn't enough to "retire" on, but they believe that by the time they want to retire in their mid 50's, the portfolio will grow to be worth over $2m (probably much more). During these years, they down-shift their careers and just concentrate on earning $80,000 a year—just enough to cover their expenses. They don't add to their portfolio, but they don't pull from it either. They simply let the portfolio grow in the background while they focus on raising their kids, travelling, and living life as they like it. 

***They Decumulate*** in their late 50's. By their late 50's, Jack and Eve's portfolio has grown to well over $2m (in real, inflation-adjusted terms). They still spend about $80,000 (in real, inflation-adjusted terms), so they know that with a "safe withdrawal rate" of 4%, they are comfortable pulling $80,000 of their portfolio each year. ($2m x 4% = $80,000). They no longer need to work—they can officially "retire."

Jack and Eve's story here is just to illustrate the differences between the Accumulation, Coast, and Decumulation phases of a CoastFIRE timeline. We've over-simplified a lot here (especially with our assumptions about the 4% rule). However, we can use Jack and Eve's story as a starting place, not a comprehensive guide. 



### Life is (ideally) not that simple.

That is the simple theory, but we know that reality is a blend. After all, the whole point of CoastFI is to live the flexible life of your choosing—not to follow some linear script.

- ***What if Eve really liked her job in her mid 30's***, so she kept working while Jack scaled back? They made only $100,000 now, spent $80,000 and invested $20,000. Is this "Accumulate" or "Coast?"
- ***What if Jack and Eve decided to become digital nomads for while?*** They stopped working completely, dropped their expenses to $30,000 a year, and pulled it from their portfolio while living in Thailand homeschooling their kids for three years. Then after that, they returned home and started working again as normal. Is that a "decumulation" phase?
- ***What if Jack and Eve want to spend their early 50's working under-paid jobs at a non-profit*** only making $25,000 a year each? They'll have $50,000 in income, and pull $30,000 from their portfolio. Is that "coast" or is it "decumulation"? 

If your life progresses in phases, how can you calculate your "CoastFI number?

You can't. 

>  There is no such thing as a CoastFI Number.

We need a tool that is more flexible. 

Specifically, over the course of their journey, Jack and Eve might be asking these questions:

- Can Jack cut back on his work if Eve keeps working?
- Can Jack and Eve take the family to Thailand for three years (pulling from their portfolio) without wrecking their plan?
- Can Jack and Eve spend their early 50's making only $50k a year while pulling from their portfolio?

These are complicated questions with a lot of variables that a simple CoastFI calculator cannot answer. Especially if Jack and Eve want to do some clever tax moves (that time in Thailand is a great opportunity for some Roth conversions!). 

Velella strives to be a conversational planning tool for Jack and Eve. 

However, SWR is still important: it is a heuristic that we can use to determine the health of our plan. This is where the Velella app comes in. 

## Plan life in "Eras"

Velella assumes that you didn't get into CoastFI so you could walk a linear path toward retirement. Instead, you're thinking about "eras" of life that each look different. As we think through these different eras, we want to know: does this take me off-track for retirement?

> I'd like to take a few years off to travel, does this take me off track for retirement?

> I'd like to cut down to part time work when my kids are young, does this take me off track to retirement?

> I'd like to quit my job and go live in Thailand working as a dive instructor for the next 10 years, does this take me off track to retirement? 

Velella takes a scenario-first approach to planning: sketch out a *scenario* of your life with its different *eras*, and see if the math adds up. If it does—go live your dreams. If it doesn't, see what you might be able to adjust. 



### Our goal is for Velella to be a conversation tool

The FIRE community is full of calculating math wonks (one of them built Velella). Our life partners' eyes tend to glaze over when we try to talk about life in terms of a spreadsheet. 

Our goal is that math wonks and normal people can sit together and discuss life plans. Feel free to play with the tool to go back and forth between the numbers and the plan. 

Don't expect to have all of the answers in an afternoon. Velella is a conversation tool: it will probably seed more questions than it answers at first. Contemplating hard questions is part of planning!



Here is how we suggest to get started:



#### Step 1: Add in your "Assumptions"

On the Assumptions screen, add:

- ***The number of adults in your family and their ages*** (make sure to mark them as "income earners"). 
- ***Your current portfolio amount.*** This is the part of your net worth that you expect to grow. It should not include equity in your house (which is illiquid) or your emergency fund (which is probably cash or cash equivalents). 

You can also add in any dependents you'll have such as children or aging parents. Dependents are just there so you can see how old everyone is over the years. 

If you have an ideal "Safe Withdraw Rate", you can add it into the assumptions. If you're not sure, stick with the default 4.7%. 



#### Step 2: Think about life

Spend some time having a conversation with your partner around the "Timeline" and try to think in detail about how you'd like your life to go. Where do you want to live? Do you want to have kids? When? Which stages of life do you want to focus on a career? Do you want to move to a major city (with higher taxes)? Do you want to move "back home" to be close to your family? 

Sketch it out on the timeline tab. If there are different options, use the "scenarios" feature to sketch some different timelines. 

Everything doesn't have to be perfect, you can always adjust later. 

#### Step 3: Add Numbers

Once you have a broad plan, start adding in numbers. Keep it simple: just add in the wages for the income earners and the estimated household expenses. Velella will estimate your federal tax (if you live in a place with state or local tax, you might want to sketch in some rough numbers). 



#### Step 4: Stand back and assess

Look at the timeline. The key thing to look at is the "C-POP." This gets a little nerdy, but the top line is this:

> When the C-POP is green, you can retire. 

As you look at the timeline, pay attention to where the C-POP starts to turn from red to yellow to green: these are important times in the evolution of your portfolio. 

(Note: if you have a few years where you have very low expenses, then your expenses rise again, it is common for the number to be green in the low-cost years, then rise up again).



***What the C-POP is measuring (and why it matters to you)***

There is a little math here. 

In Velella, C-POP stands for "Cost Percentage of Portfolio." It is the number that we get when we compare your total expenses for that year to the portfolio. For example:

- In 2030, Jack and Eve have a $1,000,000 portfolio, and they have $80,000 in expenses.
